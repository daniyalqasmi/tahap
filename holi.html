Holi

February-March (varies)

About Holi

Holi, often called the "Festival of Colors" or the "Festival of Spring," is a vibrant Hindu festival celebrated in India and Nepal. It signifies the victory of good over evil, the arrival of spring, and the opportunity to mend broken relationships. It is known for people chasing and coloring each other with dry powder (gulaal) and colored water.

Historical Background

The origins of Holi are linked to several Hindu legends. The most prominent is the story of Prahlada and his demonic father, King Hiranyakashipu. The festival culminates in the lighting of a bonfire (Holika Dahan) which symbolizes the burning of the demoness Holika, marking Prahlada's devotion and the victory of virtue over vice.

Cultural Significance

Holi holds immense cultural importance as it brings people together to celebrate and forgive. It bridges social gaps by encouraging people to meet, greet, and play with everyone, regardless of social status or religion. It is a day for enjoyment, music, dance, and sharing traditional sweets and festive drinks.
